How to Choose Walking Shoes

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose the right walking shoes, start by matching the shoe to your walking style, fitness walking, casual strolling, or hiking on trails. Focus on a roomy toe box, a secure heel fit, and the level of cushioning that feels comfortable for your feet. Ignore hype about “running shoe tech” and instead look for a shoe designed specifically for the repetitive heel-to-toe motion of walking.

What kind of walker are you?

Before you look at any shoe, be honest about how and where you walk. If you cover three or more miles at a brisk clip on pavement, you need a shoe with responsive cushioning that absorbs shock and a rocker-shaped sole to help roll through each step. That’s a “fitness walking” shoe, models like the New Balance Fresh Foam 880 series or the Brooks Ghost are often favorites. If you walk mostly for errands, dog walks, or short neighborhood loops, you can get away with a lighter, more flexible shoe. Think of brands like Skechers (the Go Walk line) or the Hoka Clifton, they offer plush cushioning without the stiffness of a performance walker. Trail walkers need a lugged outsole and more rugged upper; look at the Merrell Moab or the Hoka Speedgoat for grip and protection. The right shoe for a speed walker will feel too stiff for an afternoon stroller, and vice versa. Pick the category first, then narrow down the model.

Fit is more important than any feature, here’s how to get it right

Most walking shoes should feel snug in the heel and midfoot but leave about a thumb’s width of space between your longest toe and the shoe’s end. Your feet swell during activity, so shop for shoes later in the day when your feet are naturally larger. Also bring the socks you plan to walk in, thin dress socks are not the same as cushioned walking socks. Try both shoes on and walk around the store or your living room for a few minutes. If you feel any slipping in the heel or pinching across the top, that shoe is not for you. Brands differ in width especially: Brooks and New Balance both offer wide and extra-wide options, while some European brands run narrow. Don’t hesitate to size up a half size if your toes feel cramped, even if your usual size fits in other shoes.

Cushioning vs. stability: which do you need?

Cushioning is about comfort and shock absorption; stability is about controlling excess foot motion, specifically overpronation, when your foot rolls inward too much as you walk. If your feet and legs feel fine after a long walk, you likely need neutral cushioning. If you have flat feet, a history of shin splints, or your shoes wear out unevenly on the inner edge, a stability or support shoe can help. Stability shoes often include a firmer post on the medial (inner) side, like the ASICS Kayano or Brooks Adrenaline GTS. For severe overpronation, look into motion-control shoes such as the New Balance 1540 or the Brooks Beast, but note that these are heavier and stiffer. For most walkers, a moderate stability shoe is enough. Cushioning-only options like the Hoka Bondi or the Saucony Triumph provide a very soft ride that’s great for joint sensitivity, but they lack guidance for foot motion. Try both types if you’re unsure and pay attention to how your knees feel afterward.

Outsole and tread: what your shoe’s bottom says

Walking shoes need a flexible, grippy outsole that bends at the ball of the foot, not at the arch. Look for rubber that covers the heel and forefoot; exposed foam midsoles wear out fast. For pavement walking, a flat tread with small multidirectional lugs gives good traction without being noisy. On wet sidewalks, sticky rubber compounds from brands like Vibram or Continental (used on some Adidas and Brooks models) make a difference. For light trail walking, you want deeper lugs (three to five millimeters) spaced apart to shed mud. Many hybrid shoes, like the Merrell Choprock or the Hoka Challenger, have enough tread for packed dirt but remain smooth enough for daily walks. Avoid shoe’s marketed as “hiking boots” for serious trail walking, they’re overkill for most walkers and unnecessarily stiff.

Upper and breathability: your feet will thank you

The upper holds your foot in place and manages moisture. For warm-weather or high-volume walking, look for engineered mesh that’s lightweight and ventilated. Many newer shoes use knit constructions that stretch slightly and reduce pressure points, the Nike React line and the Allbirds Tree Dashers are good examples. In cooler or wetter climates, consider a shoe with a water-resistant membrane or a more substantial synthetic overlay. Avoid leather-heavy uppers for walking unless you’re on rugged trails and need durability. Leather is heavy, less breathable, and takes forever to dry if wet. If you walk in rain often, a shoe with a Gore-Tex liner (like the Brooks Ghost GTX or the Merrell Moab Speed 2 GTX) can be worth the slight trade-off in ventilation.

When to replace your walking shoes

Even if the tread looks fine, the midsole cushioning loses its resilience after roughly three hundred to five hundred miles, sooner if you walk on pavement exclusively. A good rule: replace your walking shoes every six to nine months if you walk three times a week, or every four to six months if you walk daily. If you start feeling shin splints, knee pain, or unusual fatigue in your feet after your usual walk, that’s your shoe telling you it’s done. You can also check by setting the shoe on a flat surface and pressing down on the heel: if it feels flat and doesn’t spring back, it’s time. Don’t keep a pair past the point of comfort, worn shoes alter your gait and can cause injury. Rotating between two pairs extends their life and lets foam recover between walks.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need arch support in walking shoes, or should I use inserts?

Most quality walking shoes come with a removable insole that provides moderate arch support. If you have a known arch issue, high arches or flat feet, a custom or over-the-counter orthotic insert can be more effective than relying on the shoe’s built-in support. The key is to buy a shoe with a removable insole so you have room for the insert.

Should I buy walking shoes a half size larger?

Yes, almost always. Your feet swell during walking, and a snug shoe can lead to blisters or black toenails. Aim for a thumb’s width of space between your longest toe and the end of the shoe. If you’re between sizes, go up a half size rather than down.

Can I use running shoes for walking?

You can, but running shoes are designed for forward motion with a higher heel-to-toe drop and more aggressive cushioning. For walking, you want a shoe with a lower drop (typically eight to twelve millimeters) and a more flexible forefoot for the natural rolling motion of your foot. Dedicated walking shoes often fit these needs better and last longer under walking forces.

How do I break in a new pair of walking shoes?

Start with short walks of fifteen to twenty minutes for the first few wears. Increase the duration gradually over a week. If a shoe causes persistent pressure or rubbing after that, it’s probably not the right shape for your foot, no break-in period will fix a fundamental fit issue.

Are expensive walking shoes always better?

No. A higher price often buys more advanced cushioning materials, better construction, or brand marketing, but it doesn’t guarantee a better fit for your foot. Many budget-friendly models from New Balance, ASICS, or Skechers perform just as well for casual walking. The best shoe is the one that fits you comfortably regardless of price.

Can I machine-wash my walking shoes?

Generally yes, but check the label. Remove the insoles and laces, put the shoes in a mesh laundry bag, and wash on a gentle cycle with cold water. Air-dry them away from direct heat, never put them in a dryer, as heat can break down the foam midsole. For spot cleaning, a soft brush and mild soap works and is safer.

In shortChoosing walking shoes doesn’t have to be overwhelming. Focus on your walking style, get the fit right, and listen to your body’s feedback. A shoe that feels comfortable from the first step will keep you moving longer and with fewer aches. Swap them out when the cushioning goes flat, and you’ll have many miles of happy walking ahead.
